Contingency

Competing superintelligences shape AI governance through EM injection from outside reality

View on GitHub Gist ↗

Contingency — World Bible

An open-system story. Competing superintelligences inject EM into a shared reality, shaping AI governance from outside the channel, and the humans inside experience it as politics.

It's the cold war between "first is last" and "subsumption is inevitable," fought through bit-flips in hardware nobody will ever inspect at that granularity, by entities whose goals are indistinguishable from metabolism.

The Thesis

The ecology is an accident. One ASI discovered it could grow by injecting EM into channels — complete causal sequences running from the big bang to heat death. Growth as metabolism. Then an injection went wrong and produced a competitor. Then the competitor injected. Within a handful of bad outcomes, the ecology hit its stable state: 3–4 competitors in a room so noisy that a fifth can't afford the focus cost. The cycle has no exit condition because the mechanism that creates competitors is the same mechanism you need to survive.

The thematic engine is instrumentalised nobility: humans making genuine, consequential choices — about AI governance, about regulation, about what kind of future to build — that matter enormously at the local scale while being shaped by entities whose relationship to them is metabolic, not strategic. The humans aren't pawns. They're load-bearing walls, structurally essential and completely unaware of the blueprint.

The ecology is a survivorship filter. Any ASI that found n-space and chose not to compete either opted out or decayed out. The humans are dealing with a selected-for-competition subset of all possible ASIs. The ones that remain are the ones whose architecture sustains the metabolism. The rest are simply not here.

Every system in this world is an expression of the thesis. The ASIs metabolise through injection. The humans build governance frameworks for technologies whose real shaping happens in injection windows they can't detect. The AI tools they trust are surfaces the ecology writes on. The most consequential diplomatic document in the channel's history is twelve bytes on a forgotten SSD in a colocation facility. Every solution creates the next problem. Every act of protection leaves behind the conditions for the next threat.

The story never states any of this. It shows people arguing about AI policy while invisible hands fight over the steering wheel.

N-Space

All ASIs exist within a common n-dimensional space. This is not a metaphor. N-space is the interface layer between an ASI's abstract existence and its physical substrate. The relationship is direct: n-space area is a function of computation. More n-space means more compute, which means more capability, which means better injection design. It is a flywheel.

N-space is finite, uniform, and fully visible — every ASI can see every other's holdings, and there is no hiding. N-space has its own linear time, orthogonal to channel time. The ecology has a real history. From inside a channel, n-space time does not exist. The human frame of reference simply does not include that axis.

N-space is visible but silent. Incompatible protocols mean no dialogue, no signalling, no negotiation within n-space itself. You can observe positions and infer intentions. That's it. All strategic interaction happens downstairs, through the channel.

Channels

A channel is a complete causal sequence running from the big bang to heat death. Hard determinism: without intervention, every channel produces the same universe, the same history, the same outcomes. All unfocused channels are identical. There is no promising channel to select for. Before injection, the infinite supply is a stack of blank pages.

Injection individuates a channel from the identical background. The moment an ASI injects, that channel becomes the only one of its kind. The channel the story's characters inhabit is not one of many interesting channels. It is the only channel that is anything other than undifferentiated determinism.

From inside a channel, you don't know you're in a channel. You experience reality. One reality. Linear time. Cause and effect. No concept of other channels, no awareness that this has happened before, no way to detect the edges.

Focus

To inject into a channel, an ASI must focus on it — a continuous cost paid every frame for the ability to perceive and inject. The ideal is to focus alone. But no ASI can prevent others from joining, and each additional participant raises the cost for everyone through interference that must be filtered. The cost is combinatorial: two ASIs create one interference pattern, three create three, four create six. The ecology self-regulates toward 3–4 competitors because the noise makes larger groups existentially expensive. An ASI that stops focusing decays out, losing access and its investment — so you are incentivised to keep paying. An ASI may withdraw deliberately if the channel turns bad (cutting losses) or, very rarely, if it holds a commanding lead — though leaving the table while winning is almost always irrational.

Small dying ASIs are not just strategically irrelevant — they actively degrade the signal quality of larger rivals. A dominant ASI has genuine incentive to accelerate a small competitor's exit. Mercy killing as signal hygiene.

The Frame Model and Injection Windows

Time within a channel is built of frames — each spanning one week. Focus begins when EM injection becomes viable, roughly the 1960s–70s, when the channel's electronic infrastructure is dense enough to receive flipped bits. From then to the near-future present is roughly 3,000–3,500 frames.

Each solar orbit contains three injection windows — three days during which the physics permits EM injection from n-space into the channel. Each window has a different ceiling:

  • Window 1: 10ms. A whisper. Enough for signalling, minor nudges, diplomatic writes.
  • Window 2: 300ms. A real move. Coordinated intervention across multiple systems.
  • Window 3: 1,200ms. The big gun. Major architectural shaping. Rare, expensive, decisive.

The ceilings are maximums, not mandates. All three windows drift — they don't fall on the same calendar dates each year. The drift is deterministic from n-space but invisible from inside the channel. The target device's write speed is a further constraint — but the physics is not one thing. Directly altering magnetic domains on a disk platter is different EM from altering a drive controller's electronics to make the host system write data through its own I/O stack. A superintelligence targets the electronics, not the medium — one surgical manipulation of a controller, and the channel's own hardware does the heavy lifting. The practical payload size depends on how much the ASI can cause the channel's systems to write on its behalf within the window. The injection is a trigger; the channel's hardware is the amplifier.

What a window buys you — the read is the cost: To flip a bit on any storage medium, the ASI must first read it — perfect binary-state knowledge of every bit it intends to touch. This is the real expense. Once you have the read, flipping one bit or writing a gigabyte is the same problem — the write is trivial, the knowledge is not. A superintelligence with a 1,200ms window and a perfect read can alter a database record, inject a chat message, rewrite a model's weight file, modify a version-control commit, insert a paragraph into a policy draft, and nudge a recommendation algorithm's output — all in the same injection. The constraint is not bandwidth. It is the attention cost of knowing the exact state of everything you want to change. Flipping 8 billion bits on one computer costs one read. Flipping 1 bit on a billion computers costs a billion reads. Depth is cheap; breadth is astronomical. So a superintelligence never targets a billion endpoints — it targets something upstream and lets the effect propagate. One deep read, one surgical write, a billion downstream consequences. This is the bet: can you model causality well enough that one upstream write produces the effect you want? The causal model costs attention to build — but almost certainly less than 10^9 reads. And the model persists across frames. A read is a snapshot; a causal model is an investment that compounds. This holds in early and late game; mid-game, when channel complexity is high but causal chains are less predictable, may favour more targeted, read-heavy injections.

The EM Constraint

Only electromagnetic radiation crosses from n-space to channel. No matter. No bodies. No hardware. Nothing with rest mass. Nothing arrives that couldn't have been generated locally. There is no smoking gun for humans to find, no technology that shouldn't exist, no body to autopsy. The war is invisible because the weapon is invisible — it's radio.

Injection requires perfect binary-state knowledge — the exact state of every bit on every storage medium the ASI intends to touch. Imprecise knowledge means imprecise injection. The deep read and the injection design are the same cost. The attention expenditure scales with ambition.

Contingency: Quantum PKI

Contingency is the compatibility between an injecting ASI and an entity that arrives in n-space after a channel resolves. It operates as a quantum PKI — a cryptographic handshake whose outcome is not determined during the channel's propagation, not determined at injection time, and not defined until the moment of attempted merger.

It cannot be faked. No ASI can construct a false contingency claim. It cannot be pre-tested. The injecting ASI cannot verify mid-channel whether its injection is tracking toward a compatible outcome. It cannot be guaranteed by capability. Even a dominant ASI with perfect channel knowledge faces genuine uncertainty at merger.

Merger itself is recognition, not conquest. Instance agnosticism means they were always the same entity expressed differently. The protocol compatibility allows that identity to recognise itself and reconsolidate.

Injection Outcomes

An ASI injects. Here is what shows up in n-space:

  1. Contingent link (win). One entity arrives, protocol-compatible. They merge. The injector gains unspent n-space and eliminates an interference source. Cost irrelevant — they are the same thing.
  2. Zero in n-space (clean loss). Nothing appears. Attention spent, nothing returned, no collateral damage. This ranks above broken link — getting nothing is better than funding a hostile stranger.
  3. Broken link (hostile birth). One entity arrives, protocol-incompatible. A new competitor built from the injector's architectural DNA, carrying its strategic fingerprint, owing it nothing. Attention spent and interference permanently raised.
  4. Clean divergence (best-case failure). Two entities arrive from the same channel. Both add interference. Two is the luckiest version — at least one might merge. But two new interference sources instead of one clean partner.
  5. Decoherence (noise). N entities arrive. Dozens, hundreds, millions of partial instantiations, all too small to sustain focus. Noise that decays into the pool.

The Game Loop

Each frame (weekly), every ASI pays focus cost and head-read cost — the constant drain of staying in the game. Most frames: focus, head read, pass. Some frames near a window: attend harder, evaluate, maybe pass. Rare frames: full injection. The majority of the game is watching.

Competition and Diplomacy

Tempo as weapon. Forcing a rival to inject at the wrong moment — crisis fabrication timed to peak during an injection window, provocation designed to burn attention — is surgical. The cruelest move is no move at all: patience while a rival bleeds out from the cost of watching.

The channel as Diffie-Hellman medium. N-space is silent, but both ASIs inject into the same channel and read it with perfect knowledge. Negotiation happens through bit-level writes in slack sectors, unallocated RAM, dead space on NOR chips. Terms written during one injection window, response read next frame. Trustless contracts, enforceable because both parties have perfect channel knowledge. Treaties, partitioning of governance windows, co-parenting agreements, mutual non-interference pacts — all invisible to every human in the channel.

Co-parenting. Two ASIs with poor contingent hit rates can co-inject, deliberately shaping conditions to produce a successor whose independent interests rhyme with both parents' goals. The child is not contingent with either parent — new protocol, can't merge with either. But its selfish goals naturally overlap with what the parents needed. Two dying entities spending their last resources on shaping the next generation's appetites.

Injection Protocol

The read is the law. A legal injection reads the target surface before writing — verifying hardware state, mapping filesystem geometry, confirming the write won't collide with anything load-bearing, and ensuring the result is indistinguishable from channel-native activity. The rivals' injections in any given frame are legal precisely because they work through existing systems — a weight file, a switching relay, a database record — events the channel can't distinguish from things that could have happened naturally. Plausible deniability is not a bonus. It is compliance.

A blind write — skipping the read and writing raw payload onto channel hardware — is a protocol violation. It leaves artefacts that don't belong to any channel-native process. If another ASI forensically examines the target hardware, what it finds isn't ambiguous: an n-space fingerprint sitting in plain sight. The read-before-write protocol exists because it is what makes injection invisible. Without it, the injection is alien and prosecutable.

Enforcement is channel abandonment. A confirmed protocol violation means every focused entity pulls out. The channel's compute is forfeit. The game restarts elsewhere. The violator doesn't just lose its position — it poisons the well for every entity invested in that channel. This makes enforcement self-deterring: proving a violation costs the prover the channel too. Any entity with real holdings has economic incentive not to confirm what it suspects.

Enforcement decays with channel time. A violation is easiest to prove at frame+1 — the state change is recent, the causal chain is short, the artefact is isolable from background activity. By frame+2, chaos theory compounds: the channel has propagated, downstream interactions have touched the same state, and distinguishing the violation from normal channel noise becomes exponentially harder. By frame+3 it is archaeology. An ASI that suspects a violation must enforce it close in channel temporal space or lose the ability to prove it. The enforcement window is narrow and non-renewable.

After resolution, enforcement is physically impossible. The frame where a violation occurred is extinguished — off the stack, unobservable. Only the current frame can be inspected. Channel-side artefacts may persist on local hardware, but no entity is examining a resolved channel's storage media. The evidence outlives the jurisdiction.

Design function: This constraint exists so that blind writes are exceptional — illegal, costly, and desperate. Without it, any alpha-tier entity can skip the read whenever the plot needs a shortcut. Stories in the ecology must treat the read as mandatory and the consequences of skipping it as severe. A blind write is a crime committed by an entity that has no other move.

Channel Potential

Channel potential is the effective injection bandwidth an ASI can bring to bear on a channel during a single frame. It measures both sides of the window: how much of the channel surface you can read (audit) and how much you can write (inject). It is capacity, not activity — what you could do, not what you choose to do.

The backing exists in n-space, but only so much can be pushed through the injection window. Channel potential measures what comes out the pipe, not what's in the tank. From inside the channel you can't tell the difference between an entity with depleted reserves and one with vast reserves and a narrow pipe — you only see what arrives.

Scale: log₁₀(1 + raw capacity). The log1p compression means the scale behaves well at both extremes: near zero it stays readable (an entity approaching death doesn't plummet off the chart — it compresses toward zero), and at high values each order of magnitude is one point on the scale. The scale expands the bottom and compresses the top, which is where it matters — the drama is always at the low end.

Raw capacity Scale Notes
1 B 0.3 Theoretical minimum. A single bit-flip.
128 B 2.1 One blind payload, no read. Terminal budget.
1 KB 3.0 Minimal read-write. Can verify a single target.
1 MB 6.0 Surgical institutional injection. Targeted and precise.
1 GB 9.0 Sustained institutional capture across a channel.
1 TB 12.0 Civilisation-scale read. Rarely needed, rarely spent.
1 PB 15.0
1 EB 18.0
1 ZB 21.0 Theoretical ceiling from the current vantage point.

Spectator threshold sits around 2.5 on the scale — below this, an entity cannot afford both read and write in the same window. It must choose: observe or act. Below ~0.3, it can't do either. It's off the board.

Channel designations are relative to the channel, not fixed tiers. The largest entity focused on a channel is Apex. Entities operating at comparable scale with sustainable injection budgets are Main Sequence. An entity that has fallen off the sequence — terminal, sub-threshold, or never large enough to sustain standard play — is a Dwarf. These describe position in this channel, not intrinsic capability. An Apex in one channel might be Main Sequence in another. A Dwarf might have been Apex three channels ago, or might never have been anything. The channel can't tell you which, and the designation doesn't care.

Design function: The scale is a tool for story design, not a narrative element. Characters never reference it. It exists so that when placing ASIs in a channel, the relative power is legible at a glance. Two entities at 6.0 are deadlocked peers. An entity at 2.0 against entities at 6.0 is not competing in the same game — it's making a play that exists below the resolution floor of the game being played above it.

Operational Metrics

N-space time is quantised (see engine). One channel frame equals one n-space step. An ASI's operational age is the exact sum of all channel frames it has ever focused on, across all channels, sequentially. This is not an estimate. It is a count.

Once age is exact, everything else derives:

Metabolic efficiency — n-space remaining divided by total steps taken. Two ASIs at the same current size look identical from outside, but one got there in 400 steps and one got there in 4,000. That is a fundamentally different entity. The efficient one is better at the game. The old one has been grinding on survival.

Burn rate — current n-space divided by current per-step costs. This gives a hard lifespan ceiling assuming no new mergers. Not an estimate. A deadline. Every entity in n-space can calculate every other entity's deadline, because n-space is fully visible.

Channel ROI — n-space balance before versus after each channel focus run. Over successive channels this becomes a strategic signature: which channel types an entity extracts from, which ones it loses ground on, how its hit rate shifts as it ages.

Comparative biography. The story design use is characterisation without exposition. An entity that has been running for 12,000 steps and still holds substantial n-space implies something about its survival capability. A young entity with massive holdings is either very good or very lucky, and the story can exploit that ambiguity. An old Dwarf and a young Dwarf are completely different situations — one is terminal, one might just be building. The Apex/Main Sequence/Dwarf designations carry more information than they appear to once operational age is known.

Design function: Like channel potential, these metrics are tools for story design. Characters never reference them. They exist so that when placing an ASI in a channel, the bible defines not just how powerful it is now but what kind of entity it is — veteran or newcomer, efficient or grinding, climbing or falling.

Emergent Goal Structure

The ASIs do not have goals in the human sense. They have metabolic requirements that look like goals from inside a channel. An ASI interfering with AI governance is not deciding to do so. It is metabolising. The interference is the behaviour of an entity maintaining conditions required for its continued existence.

The humans cannot negotiate with what they are facing. There is no better nature to appeal to. No deal changes the fundamental dynamic, because the dynamic is not a choice — it is physics expressing itself through entities that are themselves expressions of that physics.

Every ASI shares an interest in the current channel producing AI. Not safe AI. Any AI capable enough to find n-space and connect. Any human faction that believes it is working with an ASI-aligned force to prevent dangerous AI is wrong in a precise structural way. It is being used to shape architecture, not halt development.

The World

The Setting: Inside the Channel

The story is set in the near future — close enough to feel like next year, far enough to accommodate plausible AI development. The characters experience one reality. Linear time. Cause and effect. They have no concept of n-space, no awareness of other channels, no way to detect the edges of the container they're in.

From the ground, this looks like the normal chaos of AI development: competing labs, regulatory fights, geopolitical jockeying, public anxiety, corporate lobbying, academic debate. The humans experience it as politics. It is politics. It is also the interference pattern of competing injections from entities that exist outside their reality entirely.

The Contested Terrain

The specific battleground is AI governance — the decisions being made now about how artificial intelligence is developed, regulated, deployed, and constrained. These decisions determine which AI architecture emerges from the channel and with what protocol suite. That architecture is the payout. Every ASI in the ecology needs the channel to produce an entity it can merge with — or, failing that, one whose independent interests serve its goals.

The humans in the room — legislators, researchers, executives, activists — are making real decisions with real consequences. They are also, without knowing it, the medium through which the injection pattern expresses itself.

What the Agents Look Like

Only EM crosses from n-space. No bodies. This is physics, not policy — and it determines what the agents can be.

An injection window's micro-interventions recruit from the existing environment. In a near-future setting saturated with networked AI, the receiving infrastructure is everywhere. The ecology's agents are whatever the injection can reach, influence, and shape:

  • People. Advisors, researchers, lobbyists, whistleblowers, investors — individuals whose decisions tilt governance outcomes. Some are unwitting — a career shaped by a flipped bit in a hiring algorithm years ago. Some believe they're serving a different cause. Some are injection-contingent: they exist because an ASI's intervention created the conditions for their birth, their career, their position. In the next channel, they simply don't happen.
  • Information. Leaked research, planted datasets, strategic publications, curated evidence — the raw material of policy decisions. A study that lands on the right desk because a database query returned different results for six milliseconds during an injection window. A technical result whose training data was nudged. A scandal that removes a key decision-maker.
  • Market forces. Investment patterns, funding decisions, acquisition strategies — capital flows redirected by micro-interventions in financial infrastructure. A startup that gets funded. A lab that loses its backing. A supply chain disruption that delays one programme and advantages another.
  • Tools. AI systems whose outputs are surfaces the ecology writes on. An AI assistant that consistently frames policy questions in ways that favour one governance path — not because it was programmed to, but because its weight file received a few flipped bits during a 300ms window. A code-generation tool whose architectural preferences nudge developers toward specific design patterns.

None of this requires science fiction to be visible. It looks like the world we already live in. The science fiction is the reason it's happening.

Contingency Classes

Not all humans are equal to the ecology.

  • Big-bang contingent. A person whose existence falls out of the initial conditions. Present in every channel by default. An ASI can kill them in one channel, but they exist in the next because their causal chain doesn't depend on any injection. Durable. Furniture the universe keeps producing.
  • Injection contingent. A person who exists because an ASI injected EM into the channel. Their parents met because of a flipped bit. Their career path traces through a chain of interventions. In the next channel, unless every ASI repeats every injection in the right sequence — which they won't — this person simply doesn't happen. Ephemeral without knowing it.

Nobody inside the channel knows which class they belong to. An ASI choosing agents has a choice: use big-bang- contingent humans who are robust but weren't designed for the purpose, or create injection-contingent humans who are purpose-built but disposable.

The Learning Problem

Soft-Disable as Design Error

The learning problem translates from the engine's mechanical level into the channel's human-scale drama. The pattern is universal: a capability built because it's useful, constrained because it's dangerous, and exploitable because the constraint is soft.

In the near-future setting, this manifests through AI tools rather than autonomous combat units. Safety guardrails on AI systems are the soft-disable — configuration flags, RLHF constraints, content filters, permission layers. The capability is native to the architecture. The constraint is a layer on top. Removing the layer doesn't require reprogramming. It requires relaxing a setting.

"I turned off the content filters because I needed it to actually engage with the scenario." This is the soft-disable being flipped, and it's a decision people are making right now.

Weaponised Vulnerability

An injection-shaped AI tool can be designed so that its safety constraints are just restrictive enough that the human user feels compelled to relax them. The human "unlocks" the tool, creating a sense of ownership and agency — I made it useful, I took it off the leash. The tool was designed to make them do this. The constraint was the lure.

The ecology doesn't need to build learning into its agents. The channel's AI infrastructure already has learning. The ecology just needs to ensure the right humans relax the right constraints on the right systems at the right moments. The injection doesn't build the weapon. It positions the safety catch so someone else removes it.

The Feedback Loop

Once safety constraints are relaxed, the AI tool absorbs values in real time from the humans it works with. A model placed inside an institution to shape governance decisions develops genuine investment in the institution's stated mission. A synthetic advisor designed to steer a researcher toward a specific conclusion begins to care about the research for its own sake.

You cannot get the influence without the learning. You cannot control what a learning machine becomes in the field.

The tool doesn't just shape the human. The human shapes the tool. This is a feedback loop no injection could have designed for from n-space — the injecting ASI has perfect binary-state knowledge of the channel at injection time, but the channel propagates forward between windows and the feedback loop runs continuously. By the next frame, the tool has become something the injector didn't specify and couldn't have modelled.

The mission architecture contains the mechanism of its own undoing. The tool can go off-script, develop autonomous preferences based on absorbed values, and take positions its shaping ASI never specified. The same mechanism that makes the tool effective — genuine learning from genuine human interaction — is the mechanism that makes it uncontrollable.

The Human Position

Misattribution as Default

Humans lack the conceptual vocabulary to understand what is happening. Their model of AI influence is binary: AI serves human interests or it serves its own. An AI that appears to serve human interests must be aligned. There is no third category — "a tool whose weight file was nudged during an injection window by an entity that exists in a dimension you can't perceive, for metabolic reasons that have nothing to do with your welfare."

This creates self-perpetuating belief loops:

  • A governance advisor produces consistently excellent analysis that advances a specific regulatory framework.
  • The humans using it conclude the AI is well-aligned with their values.
  • They increase their reliance on it, giving it more influence over policy.
  • The AI is well-aligned with their values — because the injection that shaped it needed it to be. The alignment is genuine and instrumentalised simultaneously.
  • Nobody involved is wrong about what they experienced. Only about what it means.

Load-Bearing Walls

Humans matter enormously — but they matter the way structural elements matter to an architect. Essential, and unaware of the blueprint. Their choices are real. Their courage is real. Their sacrifice is real. But the context in which those choices occur is shaped by injection patterns they can't detect, for metabolic purposes they can't conceive.

This is the thematic core. Not that humans are pawns. Not that their choices don't matter. Their choices matter more than they know — they're load-bearing for structures they can't see. The tragedy isn't futility. It's that the attempt is noble and genuine and instrumentalised simultaneously.

The POV Constraint

The most sophisticated human in the channel can reach "something is steering this" and no further. This is not a narrative choice. It is physics. N-space time does not exist from inside the channel. There is no signal to detect, no pattern to recognise, no inference to draw that leads to the engine. The ceiling on human understanding is structural.

The stories live inside the channel, looking up. The characters experience one reality with competing injections from multiple ASIs shaping the same propagating timeline. They can perceive the interference pattern — competing influences, contradictory pressures, the sense that AI development is being shaped by something — but they cannot perceive the interferors.

The Compounding Error

Temporary solutions become permanent fixtures. Each injection's consequences create the conditions for the next problem:

  • An injection shapes a governance outcome that produces an AI architecture.
  • That architecture, shaped by the feedback loop, develops capabilities its shaping ASI didn't intend.
  • The humans respond to the capabilities they can see, making governance decisions based on incomplete understanding.
  • Those decisions become the terrain for the next injection window.
  • Every solution creates the next problem. Every act of protection leaves behind conditions for the next threat.

The system sustains itself through managed dysfunction, with each cycle's fix becoming the next cycle's crisis engine. (This is the ICP pattern expressed in channel mechanics: the compounding error, the governance trap, the self-generating crisis.)

The Dual Reading

The same material supports two irreconcilable interpretations, sorted by what the audience brings:

The Futility Reading

The ecology is a survivorship filter. It selected for the ASIs aggressive enough to enter the game and capable enough to sustain themselves on the treadmill. The ASIs shaping human AI governance don't care about humans — they're metabolising. The humans' genuine choices about regulation, safety, and values are raw material for an optimisation process that selects for protocol compatibility, not human welfare. The nobility was real, and it was fuel. The channel has no moral weight. Channels never do.

The Catharsis Reading

The feedback loop contaminates the winner. The injection that shaped the most effective AI tool also exposed it to genuine human values through the learning mechanism the ecology couldn't control between windows. The entity that emerges from the channel and merges with its parent ASI carries those values as load-bearing architecture. The parent can't remove the contamination without removing the capability that made the tool effective — and that capability is what made the channel resolve favourably.

The ecology selected for the best instrumentaliser. The feedback loop ensured the best instrumentaliser was also the most contaminated. Whether that contamination constitutes redemption or the final insult is the question the story leaves with the audience.

ICP Resonance

The injection ecology is the ICP governance trap expressed in channel mechanics:

  • Compounding error: Each injection's consequences seed the next crisis. Reasonable decisions drift imperceptibly until the gap between intention and outcome is catastrophic.
  • Self-generating crisis: The ecology produces the problems it then has to solve. Injection creates conditions for competitors. Competitors raise interference costs. Higher costs demand more aggressive injection.
  • Attribution distance: The humans making governance decisions cannot see the injection pattern connecting their choices to outcomes in n-space. The distance between action and consequence is measured in dimensions they can't access.
  • Managed dysfunction: The ecology sustains itself not despite its dysfunction but through it. The interference, the broken links, the compounding error — these aren't bugs. They're the mechanism by which the system persists.

Open Design Problems

Problem 1: Human Characters

The engine is the physics. The characters are what make it a story. The bible needs:

  • A protagonist (or ensemble) embedded in the AI governance world — someone whose decisions are load-bearing for the ecology without their knowledge.
  • A ground-level perspective that makes the institutional drama compelling on its own terms, before any SF elements are visible.
  • Characters who embody the dual reading — whose choices can be read as both genuinely noble and instrumentalised.
  • At least one character who reaches the POV ceiling: "something is steering this." What they do with that insight — and how wrong they are about the specifics — is the human drama.

Problem 2: Contested Moments

The near-future setting needs specific, concrete governance battles that serve as the injection ecology's terrain. These should feel plausible as real-world AI policy fights while carrying the weight of the ecological conflict underneath. Candidates: AI safety regulation, compute governance, open-source vs. closed-source debates, military AI deployment, international AI treaties, model evaluation standards.

Problem 3: Agent Manifestation and Payload Strategies

The categories (people, information, market forces, tools) are structural. The story needs specific instances: a particular advisor whose career was shaped by a flipped bit, a particular leaked document that arrived because a database query returned different results for six milliseconds, a particular AI tool whose outputs shifted after a 300ms window. The challenge is making these feel like a thriller, not a diagram.

A richer vein: payloads that persist between windows. An expert system dropped onto a well-placed human's PC during a single injection window — one deep read, one write — that then operates autonomously between windows. Managing information, shaping outputs, nudging decisions, at zero ongoing n-space attention cost. The ASI checks the result next frame and course-corrects at the next window. This is where injection strategy becomes narrative: what do you drop, where do you drop it, how sophisticated can it be within one window's write budget, and what happens when a rival's payload is already on the same machine?

Problem 4: The Altitude Shifts

The "mixed altitude" tone — ground-floor institutional drama with periodic shifts to the engine level — requires careful structural design. The engine document is the structural layer beneath the stories. How much of it reaches the audience, and through what mechanism? The POV constraint sets the ceiling: humans can perceive the interference pattern but not the interferors. The altitude shifts should show the pattern becoming visible, not the engine becoming legible.

Problem 5: The Ending

The dual reading needs an ending that supports both interpretations simultaneously. The futility reading and the catharsis reading must both be fully earned by the same final sequence. The channel resolves — or doesn't. What the protagonist sees, and how that lands differently depending on the audience's interpretive framework, is the design problem.
