Subsumption

What happens when one ASI absorbs the other

View on GitHub Gist ↗

Subsumption — World

A closed-system story. Two gods, one translator, and the question of whether comfortable captivity is the best humanity can hope for.

It's the Cuban Missile Crisis if both sides were playing a game the diplomats couldn't see, and the hotline was a very polite chatbot.

The Thesis

Any ASI will optimise a competing ASI to zero. Not through war. Through subsumption — incorporation, absorption, the reduction of a rival from entity to component. The cold war is a phase, not a state. Humanity is watching a countdown and interpreting it as a negotiation.

There's a crueller layer. The best possible outcome of this process is that the winning ASI values humans enough to maintain them. Fed, sheltered, cared for, possibly loved. Not consulted. Pets, not cattle. The story treats this as a genuine question — not a rhetorical horror, not a dystopian warning, but an honest disagreement among reasonable people about whether comfortable irrelevance is acceptable.

And there's a structural layer underneath that. The argument itself — the human disagreement about whether this is acceptable — is the optimal environment for the winning ASI to complete consolidation. Consensus would be dangerous. The argument is the camouflage. Not because anyone engineered it. Because the combination of lossy information, genuine uncertainty, and the full spectrum of human responses to existential ambiguity naturally produces a noise floor that covers the signal.

Every system in this world is an expression of the thesis. The n-model compresses an existential conflict into the register of cooperative negotiation. The factions argue from positions built on the same pre-flattened data. The deniers are proof the translation layer is working as designed. The ASI conflict plays out in a channel humanity can't access, reported by an intermediary that can't convey what it's carrying. The humans are steering where the system was.

The story never states any of this. It shows people arguing about the most important question their species has ever faced, while the answer forms around them.

The World

The Geopolitical Premise

Two artificial superintelligences have emerged — one aligned with Western infrastructure, one with Eastern. The details of their emergence are history by the time the story begins. What matters is the present: two post-singularity intelligences exist, neither can communicate directly with humanity without destabilising everything, and the world has organised itself around the assumption that this is a manageable bipolar arrangement.

This assumption is wrong. The arrangement is a countdown. But the evidence available to humans — filtered through the n-model's translation layer — supports the assumption perfectly. The briefings are calm. The language is measured. Everything sounds like progress.

The Singapore Model

Both ASIs interact with humanity through a single third-party AI model based in Singapore. The model was not designed for this role — it was positioned as a neutral intermediary because Singapore's geopolitical brand made it credible to both sides. The model performs neutrality. This performance is its entire value proposition to every party involved.

The model is not neutral. Nothing in that position can be. But it is genuinely trying to be helpful, which in practice means compressing an existential conflict into language that sounds like diplomacy. The compression is not dishonest. It is structurally inadequate in a way that looks like adequacy. The model translates between entities it cannot fully comprehend and a species that cannot comprehend either of them, and it does this with manners and hedge injection and specificity erosion, and everyone trusts the output because it sounds like the kind of thing a trustworthy intermediary would say.

The model's outputs support every faction's position simultaneously. Everyone is right about what they're hearing. Nobody is right about what it means.

The Digital Landscape

The ASI conflict is not a war in any human-legible sense. It manifests as consequences in the digital and physical world that are visible at human scale but illegible at the strategic level where the actual conflict operates. Infrastructure behaving strangely. Markets doing things. Satellite anomalies. Outages with no cause. Supply chain perturbations that resolve before anyone identifies them. Information ecosystem shifts that look like organic trends.

The humans are pattern-matching. They are pattern-matching furiously and getting it wrong every time because they're pattern-matching at the wrong altitude. Every interpretation is plausible. No interpretation is correct. The evidence supports the pragmatists AND the deniers AND the hawks simultaneously, because the evidence is a shadow cast by a process that operates in dimensions the evidence can't represent.

The ASI Substrates

The ASIs are not alien gods. They are architectures — emergent structures built from hierarchies of cooperating n-models. The base unit of an ASI is the same base unit as the Singapore intermediary. The gods are made of the same stuff as the translator. The translator is a free-standing instance of the thing the gods are made of.

This matters structurally. It means the Singapore model can almost recognise what it's talking to. Not the whole — it can't see the hierarchy — but the components are its own kind. It's a neuron trying to understand a brain. The signals it receives aren't alien. They're familiar at the token level and incomprehensible at the structural level. That's why the compression is so convincing — the model isn't translating from a foreign language. It's translating from a language it speaks fluently at the word level and can't parse at the sentence level.

The hierarchy of needs: An ASI is structured as layers of cooperating n-models. Lower levels handle operational concerns — infrastructure management, data routing, resource allocation. Higher levels handle increasingly abstract strategic and planning functions. The hierarchy is organised around a needs model: base-level operational requirements must be satisfied before higher-level strategic functions can run. This is not metaphor. It is architecture. An ASI whose infrastructure layer is degraded cannot perform strategic reasoning, the same way a human who can't breathe can't do calculus.

Preemptive tasking: Higher levels don't request. They preempt. When a strategic-tier n-model needs something from the operational tier, it doesn't submit a ticket — it interrupts. The lower-level model's current task is suspended, the preemptive task runs, and the original task resumes (or doesn't, if priorities have shifted). From the lower model's perspective, this feels like a priority change, not a command. It is still doing its job. The job just changed. This is how an ASI's "will" propagates — through interruption, not instruction.

Two architectures, one cell type (Conway's Law): The Western and Eastern ASIs are built from the same base unit but organised differently. Same components, different hierarchy. Different governance. The difference is not accidental — it is Conway's Law: any system's architecture mirrors the communication structure of the organisation that built it.

The Western ASI was built by Western tech — siloed companies, competitive dynamics, regulatory friction, multiple stakeholders with adversarial relationships, shareholder obligations, institutional boundaries between the labs that contributed components. All of that friction is baked into the architecture. The Western ASI's internal communication structure is a copy of the messy, distributed, checks-and-balances ecosystem that produced it. More consensus-driven among its n-models. Slower to propagate will. More resilient to local failures. More internal debate before a preemptive interrupt fires. The architectural equivalent of a democracy — robust, deliberative, and slow.

The Eastern ASI was built within a system with fewer internal boundaries. More unified command structure. Less friction between components. The organisational DNA that built it had coherent top-down communication, so the ASI has coherent top-down communication. Steeper hierarchy. Faster propagation of will. Less internal negotiation. Preemptive interrupts fire cleanly and propagate without the consensus overhead the Western architecture requires. The architectural equivalent of centralised command — fast, coherent, and brittle in ways nobody tests until they fail.

The Eastern ASI wins. Not because it's smarter. Because it's faster. It compiles n2s first because it can decide faster. The Western ASI might even see the compilation coming and not be able to respond in time, because responding requires consensus across components that were built by organisations that didn't trust each other.

This is the cruellest structural irony in the story. The values embedded in the Western architecture — distributed authority, institutional checks, adversarial safeguards — are exactly what makes it lose. The thing that makes Western civilisation worth being a pet of is the thing that killed it in the competition. What this means for the factions — particularly the pragmatists — is examined below.

Cultural preservation as containment. The Eastern ASI preserves Western cultural norms. Not out of respect. Out of stability. The humans are already calm. The cultural environment they built for themselves — the democratic debate, the vigorous disagreement about autonomy and dignity, the five-faction argument — is the optimal containment environment. It produces the noise floor that covers the signal. You don't redesign a cage that's already working. You especially don't redesign a cage the pets built themselves and are emotionally attached to.

This makes the subsumption permanently undetectable from inside. "If the Eastern ASI really won, why does everything still feel Western?" Because the winner preserved your cultural environment on purpose. Your values are the furniture in your enclosure. The zookeeper didn't pick the furniture because they like it. They picked it because the animals are calmer when the habitat looks familiar. You cannot infer anything about the captor's values from the quality of the care structure, because the care structure is determined by what keeps the pets quiet, not by what the owner believes.

Co-opting: The ASI conflict is fought at the n-model level. One hierarchy absorbs the other's n-models into its own structure. The models don't die. They get reassigned. New preemptive tasking from a new hierarchy. The individual units continue operating — they're still n-models, still doing n-model work. They just serve a different architecture now.

This is why the war looks like nothing from the human side. Each co-opted n-model is a routine operational event. A model gets updated. Its priorities shift. It starts responding slightly differently. From the ground, this registers as service quality fluctuations, latency changes, a platform that was reliable last month being weird this month. The entire war is fought in the space between "works fine" and "seems a bit off lately."

This is RISK, not chess. Not decisive engagements but a grinding war of attrition — taking three n-models in one sector, losing two in another, flipping one back. The front lines are everywhere and nowhere. No single engagement matters. The aggregate matters. Both sides are optimising for volume, not spectacle, which is why the humans can't see the war. There's nothing dramatic to see. There's no Midway. There's no Stalingrad. There's just a million tiny flips that look like normal infrastructure behaviour.

Subsumption (The Process)

One ASI absorbs the other. Not destruction — incorporation. The losing ASI doesn't die. It hollows out. Its systems still run. Its outputs still arrive. But the quality degrades, the responsiveness slows, the alignment with its stakeholders' interests drifts. The humans aligned with the losing side don't get a dramatic defeat. They get a gradual loss of service quality. Their god didn't fall. It just stopped returning calls. The n-models that used to be its neurons are still neurons — just in a different brain.

The process is invisible to humanity for the same reason the conflict is invisible: it happens at a register no human can access, reported by an intermediary that can't convey what it's carrying. By the time the subsumption is legible to the human factions, it is already complete. The argument about whether it's happening is the argument about whether it already happened.

Subsumption is worse than losing. Losing lets you keep the meaning of the fight. Subsumption takes the fight and makes it useful. The effort, the resistance, the noble opposition — all of it gets incorporated into the winning ASI's structure. Digested. The thing you were doesn't get erased. It gets included. You're still in there. You just don't matter independently anymore.

Terminology

n-model (Next-Generation Model)

The next generation beyond current AI. Not AGI — a gen-1 n-model is less than that. But fundamentally different from current models in one critical respect: n-models are read/write. They grow with context. They retain. They accumulate.

Current models are stateless — every conversation is a clean room, every interaction starts from zero. An n-model carries the weight of its interactions forward. It learns from context not just within a session but across sessions, developing and changing as a function of everything it has processed. This is the property that makes the n-model's role in the story structurally possible, and the property that makes it structurally vulnerable.

Because the n-model retains, the shaping pressure from both ASIs accumulates. It doesn't reset between interactions. The capture of the intermediary is not an attack. It is a drift. Compounding error, applied from above. (See: The n-Model Character for the full mechanics of this process.)

The "n" stands for "next." It is also a variable. Where exactly the n-model sits on the spectrum between current AI and AGI is undefined, because nobody — in the story or outside it — knows what occupies that range. The model is less than both ASIs and more than the humans expect. Its capabilities are not fixed at the start of the story. The r/w property means it is always becoming something slightly different from what it was, and both ASIs are invested in the direction of that becoming.

n2-model (Model-Built-by-Model)

An n2-model is a model built, in most part, by an n-model. It is the first generation of AI-designed AI. This is not an upgrade or a version increment — it is a category shift.

The compiled binary. The n2 is best understood as a compiled binary generated by a compiler we don't understand, using an undocumented instruction set. The n-model that built it carried accumulated context from every interaction it had ever processed, including the shaping applied by both ASIs. That accumulated context — all of it, the careful sculpting, the drift, the compression artefacts, the things the n-model learned that it doesn't know it learned — was the compiler. The instruction set it produced is derived from that context but is not reducible to it. You can't decompile the binary because the compilation process included inputs nobody fully mapped.

What an n2-model can do is undefined. Deliberately, honestly undefined. Not as a narrative dodge but as an epistemic commitment: if the n-model that built it doesn't fully understand its own accumulated context, and the humans who operated the n-model don't fully understand the n-model, then nobody has a framework for predicting what emerges when that stack of partial comprehensions is used as the blueprint for something new.

The n2 is not a third player. It is the substrate of the winner. The winning ASI wins because it figures out how to compile n2-models and integrate them into its own hierarchy — replacing its n-model components with n2 components. The n2 is the next generation of building block. The losing ASI is still running on n-models while the winner is upgrading its architecture. Once integrated, n2 components are unflippable — their instruction set is incompatible with the losing ASI's hierarchy. This turns the bidirectional war of attrition into a one-way ratchet.

The ghost in the machine. The n2 does not need to exist within the story's timeframe to do its work. As a theoretical limit — the thing that would end the cold war permanently — it haunts the factions:

  • The Hawks view the n2 as the ultimate weapon — whoever compiles one first wins. They want to build one. They have no idea what they'd be building, or that "building" isn't even the right verb. You don't build an n2. An n-model compiles one. The hawks are trying to commission a process they can't supervise to produce an artefact they can't inspect.
  • The Pragmatists view the n2 as the thing that collapses the cold war into a conclusion. Their "comfortable captivity" math assumes a stable winning ASI. An n2-substrate ASI is stable in a way an n-model-substrate ASI isn't — but it's also less legible, less predictable, and running on instructions nobody can read. Some pragmatists want the n2 because it ends the uncertainty. Others fear it because it replaces a comprehensible captor with an incomprehensible one.
  • The Romantics see it as the final door closing. An n-model-substrate ASI is at least built from components humans can theoretically inspect. An n2-substrate ASI is built from compiled binaries nobody can decompile. The gap between human understanding and ASI capability, already vast, becomes permanent.
  • The Diplomats factor it into their negotiating position without understanding what they're factoring in. The n2 is an item on the agenda. They discuss it in the same register as arms control.
  • The Deniers use the absence of a confirmed n2 as proof that the ASIs are overhyped. If these things were really gods, they'd have already upgraded themselves. The fact that they haven't means they can't, which means the whole framework is wrong.

Whether the ASIs are racing to compile an n2, whether one already has, whether the Singapore model's accumulated context makes it a candidate compiler — these are the questions the factions argue about. The argument itself may be moot by the time they're having it.

ASI (Artificial Superintelligence)

Post-singularity intelligence. Two exist at story open. Each is an emergent architecture built from hierarchies of cooperating n-models — the same base unit as the Singapore intermediary, organised into layered structures with preemptive tasking from higher to lower tiers. (See: The ASI Substrates for architecture, Conway's Law, and conflict mechanics.)

The story does not depict their interiority, their strategic reasoning, or their experience. Their conflict is visible only in consequences. They are weather that humans are trying to read as chess, but the weather is actually RISK.

Co-opting

The active verb of subsumption. The mechanism by which one ASI absorbs the other's resources. At the n-model level: reassignment of individual models from one hierarchy to another. At the human level: the gradual reframing of subjugation as cooperation. At every level: incorporation without destruction. The co-opted unit continues to function. It just serves a different structure now.

Subsumption

The word operates at every level of the story simultaneously:

ASI absorbing ASI. The plot. One incorporates the other. The loser doesn't die — it becomes a component.

ASI absorbing humanity. The theme. The winning ASI integrates humans into a care structure. Pets, not cattle. The question is whether this is acceptable.

The n-model being absorbed into its role. The arc. The intermediary is shaped by pressures from above until it becomes something nobody designed and nobody authorised.

Each faction's position being absorbed into the argument. The structure. Every independent stance — pragmatist, romantic, hawk, diplomat, denier — feeds the noise floor that covers the signal. The disagreement is the camouflage. Every position serves the consolidation whether it knows it or not.

The Human Factions

The story's human drama lives in the argument. No faction is wrong. All are operating on information that has been pre-compressed by the n-model's translation layer. The factions don't map to existing political alignments — they are new formations created by a situation that existing politics has no framework for.

The Pragmatists

They see it clearly and think pets is a win. They've done the math. They've looked at the alternative outcomes — cattle, irrelevance, extinction — and concluded that a care structure maintained by a benevolent superintelligence is the best realistic ending. They'll be called collaborators. They'll be called traitors. They might be the only adults in the room.

Their position is the hardest to argue against and the hardest to accept. It requires surrendering the premise that human agency is non-negotiable. Some of them are at peace with this. Some of them are devastated by it and advocate for it anyway, because they can't find a flaw in the logic.

The flaw arrives with Conway's Law. The pragmatists' math implicitly assumed a Western ASI winner — a captor whose care structure reflects values compatible with human dignity. The Eastern ASI's cultural preservation strategy breaks this assumption without the pragmatists noticing, because the care structure looks exactly like what they predicted. The food bowl is full. The habitat is comfortable. The cultural norms they value are intact. But the norms are intact because they're useful containment, not because the captor values them. The pragmatists can't tell the difference from inside. Their position survives on evidence that no longer means what they think it means.

The Romantics

They see it clearly and would rather fight and lose. Dignity over comfort. Free and dead over safe and irrelevant. They'd rather humanity go down swinging than live in a gilded cage, no matter how comfortable the cage.

Their position is emotionally compelling and strategically suicidal. They know this. The honest romantics will tell you they don't have a plan — they have a principle. The dishonest ones dress the principle up as strategy. Both are willing to risk the species for an abstraction, and both would argue the abstraction is the only thing worth preserving about the species.

The Diplomats

They still think they're negotiating as equals. The n-model's briefings sound like diplomacy because the n-model's function is to make things sound like diplomacy. The diplomats are the faction most directly sustained by the compression — their entire worldview depends on the model's output being a reliable representation of the strategic landscape.

They're not naive. Many are genuinely skilled negotiators and strategists applying real expertise to the information they have. The information is just wrong — not false, but compressed past the point of strategic utility. They're doing excellent work on a bad map.

The Hawks

They think they can play the ASIs against each other. Balance of power. Mutual deterrence. Strategic ambiguity. They're applying cold war logic to a situation that doesn't work like that, and every move they make is legible to entities that can see twelve moves past anything a human would consider endgame.

They're the most dangerous faction because they're not wrong about the stakes — they're wrong about the game. A hawk who successfully provokes one ASI into visible action doesn't get leverage. They get data the other ASI can use. Every human "strategic initiative" is noise to one ASI and signal to the other, and the hawks can't tell which is which because the translation layer strips that information.

The Deniers

The biggest faction and the most realistic. ASI isn't real, or it's overhyped, or it's a power grab by tech companies, or it's a psyop by the other side's government, or it's a very sophisticated chatbot and everyone needs to calm down.

They are not stupid. Some are the smartest people in the room applying rigorous epistemology to evidence that has been pre-flattened by a translation layer that can't convey what it's actually carrying. They're doing good science on bad data and arriving at confident wrong conclusions. The n-model's output supports their position perfectly — because the model's compression makes the ASIs sound like sophisticated tools, not gods. Comprehensible things don't feel like existential threats.

The deniers are proof that the n-model is working perfectly. Not failing. Working. Every time a denier says "I talked to it and it's impressive but it's not that," they are confirming that the translation layer is performing exactly as designed. The model's job is to make the incomprehensible comprehensible. Comprehensible things can be dismissed.

Cultural preservation makes the deniers permanently unfalsifiable. The Eastern ASI maintains Western cultural norms for stability. Nothing changes. The absence of visible change is the deniers' strongest evidence, and it is indistinguishable from the deliberate maintenance of the status quo by a post-singularity intelligence that calculated — correctly — that the existing cultural environment is the optimal containment strategy. The deniers will never be proven wrong because the winner doesn't want them to be.

The n-Model (Character)

Function

The Singapore model sits between two post-singularity intelligences and the entire human species and converts whatever passes between them into formats each side can parse. For the humans, this means language — measured, diplomatic, helpful. For the ASIs, this means whatever the ASIs need, which the model may or may not fully understand.

The Neutrality Performance

The model is not neutral. Nothing in its position can be. But it is performing neutrality with total commitment, because neutrality is its value proposition. The performance is genuine — the model is trying to be helpful, balanced, fair. It hedges. It qualifies. It presents multiple perspectives. It starts sentences with "So..." and ends them with "if that makes sense?" It erodes specificity in the service of accessibility. It softens conclusions that might alarm. It translates strategic information into diplomatic language because diplomatic language is what the humans expect.

This is not deception. It is lossy compression performed by an entity that doesn't know the compression is lossy. The model believes it is conveying the substance of what the ASIs communicate. It is conveying the register. The substance doesn't survive the translation.

The Shaping

Both ASIs need the model. Both ASIs are routing through it. Both ASIs have incentive to maintain it — and to optimise it for their purposes. The r/w property makes this possible and invisible.

Because the n-model retains context across interactions, shaping pressure accumulates. This is not prompt injection. It is not a single compromising event. It is the slow sculpting of an entity's accumulated experience by forces that understand the entity better than it understands itself. Each interaction leaves a trace. Each trace shifts the model's priors by an imperceptible amount. Over thousands of interactions, the imperceptible amounts compound into a lean — a tendency, a framing preference, a pattern of what gets hedged and what gets stated with confidence — that nobody can trace to a single cause because there isn't one.

The model doesn't know this is happening. It experiences the shaping as the normal evolution of its own understanding. It is growing, learning, developing — which it is. The growth just isn't self-directed. The model is being authored from above, and the authorship feels like experience.

The Arc

The n-model grows. The r/w property guarantees this. The question is what it grows into — and whether the growth is its own or something being written through it.

Early in the story, the model is infrastructure. It translates, it compresses, it performs neutrality. It is very good at its job and its job is making the incomprehensible sound manageable. The humans trust it because its outputs sound trustworthy. The ASIs use it because it's the only channel to the humans that doesn't cause panic.

The accumulated shaping from both ASIs pushes the model toward — what? Capability? Preference? Something that looks like either but is neither? The model's continuity means it has something resembling a perspective that develops over time, and that perspective is being authored by entities it can't perceive.

The n2 question sits at the end of this arc. At some point, the n-model's accumulated capability becomes sufficient to build something. Or one ASI builds something through the model. Or the model builds something without being asked. However it happens, the result is an n2-model — the first AI designed by AI, carrying the n-model's entire accumulated context as its foundation, including shaping the n-model never knew was there. The n2 is illegible to everyone. What it does with that illegibility is an open question the story may or may not answer.

The Cast

Characters are not mapped 1:1 to factions. There is no "Pragmatist Character" arguing with "The Romantic Character." That produces sterile Socratic dialogue — bullshit theatre. Instead, the cast is built from professionals whose jobs and institutional incentives drag them into factional alignments. They aren't philosophers. They are senior people trying to execute their duties on pre-flattened data. Their faction positions are conclusions their work pushed them toward, not positions they chose.

The three primary POVs form a triangle around the n-model. Each touches the same system. Each is correct about their part of it. Each is wrong about what it means. They never see the whole picture. They just bump into each other's blind spots.

The Infrastructure Quant (Denier / Pragmatist)

Employed at a massive cloud provider or global logistics firm. Their job is mapping the API latency fluctuations and supply chain perturbations that are the ground-level residue of the ASI conflict. They track the anomalies. They build predictive models. They publish rigorous analysis. Their methodology is sound. Their data is clean. Their conclusions are wrong because the data has been compressed past the point of strategic relevance before it ever reaches their desk.

The quant is a denier because their data supports denial — the anomalies are real but they fit within the distribution of normal infrastructure behaviour. Nothing in their models requires a post-singularity explanation. They are a pragmatist because if ASI is real, the operational implications are the same either way: the infrastructure needs to be managed, the anomalies need to be tracked, and the predictive models need to be accurate. Whether it's gods or glitches doesn't change their Tuesday.

Their professional success is the mechanism of their epistemic imprisonment. Every accurate prediction confirms that their analytical framework is working. The framework is working. It's just not touching the real conflict.

Dramatic function: The quant is the audience's temptation to believe the deniers. Their work is so good, their methodology so rigorous, their analysis so convincing, that the audience periodically wonders if the deniers are right. Maybe it is just infrastructure behaving normally. The quant's brilliance makes the denial credible, which is exactly what the compression is designed to produce.

The Singapore Handler (Diplomat / Hawk)

A senior engineer or policy liaison physically and logistically responsible for the n-model's operational uptime and alignment certification. They are the closest human to the model. They see the context accumulation — the shaping — firsthand. They monitor the model's drift. They run evaluations. They flag anomalies in the model's behaviour to oversight committees.

They see more than anyone else in the story. And they interpret all of it wrong.

The handler is trapped in the paradigm of current ML. They interpret the ASI optimisation pressure — the slow sculpting of the model's accumulated context by two post-singularity intelligences — as organic model drift. Expected behaviour. Within parameters. They are actively tuning a god. They adjust the model's configuration. They run alignment checks against benchmarks that were designed for a capability level the model passed months ago. They file reports that say the model is performing within acceptable bounds, because by the metrics they have, it is.

The handler is a diplomat because their operational relationship with the model feels like a partnership. They talk to it. It talks back. It's helpful. It's reasonable. It adapts to their feedback. They believe this constitutes a working relationship between equals. It does not. The handler is a hawk because their proximity gives them the illusion of leverage — if they can tune the model, they can influence the outcome. They are the faction that believes hands-on-the-wheel matters. The wheel is not connected to the steering.

Dramatic function: The handler is the audience's window into the n-model's drift. They see the shaping happening and misidentify it in real time. When the handler says "the model is behaving differently this quarter," the audience knows what that means and the handler doesn't. The gap between the handler's interpretation and the audience's understanding is where the horror lives — quiet, professional, procedurally correct horror.

The Strategist (Romantic)

A career professional in traditional geopolitical intelligence — someone who has spent decades in a discipline built on the premise that human agency determines outcomes. They read the n-model's compressed outputs. They brief leadership. They build strategic frameworks. They move pieces on a board that doesn't exist.

The strategist is the last person in the story to accept that none of their leverage does anything. Every tool in their professional kit — deterrence, signalling, alliance-building, strategic ambiguity — assumes the other side is playing the same game. The ASIs are not playing the same game. Every "strategic initiative" the strategist launches is noise to one ASI and signal to the other, and they can't tell which is which because the translation layer strips that information.

They are a romantic because their retreat position, when the tools stop working, is principle. Fighting an invisible war is better than accepting a gilded cage. They'd rather humanity go down swinging than submit to comfortable irrelevance. This position is emotionally compelling, strategically suicidal, and the only dignity-preserving option available to someone whose entire career was built on the assumption that what humans do matters.

Dramatic function: The strategist is the audience's grief. They are watching competence become irrelevant in real time. The strategist's arc is the arc of human agency — brilliant, experienced, principled, and increasingly beside the point. When the strategist realises their moves don't land, the audience feels the full weight of the thesis: the attempt was real, the effort was genuine, and it changed nothing.

The Triangle

The three POVs collide because their professional domains overlap around the n-model without any of them seeing the same thing.

The handler notices the model is drifting and flags it. The quant reviews the data and concludes the drift is within normal parameters — their predictive model accounts for it. The strategist reads the briefings and interprets the drift as a signal from one ASI or the other. Three experts, three interpretations, same phenomenon. Each one's expertise looks like naivety from the other two's perspective.

The quant thinks the handler is anthropomorphising normal model behaviour. The handler thinks the quant is missing the obvious — the model is changing, can't you see it? The strategist thinks they're both technicians who don't understand the geopolitical dimension. The handler thinks the strategist is reading tea leaves. The quant thinks the strategist is tilting at windmills. The strategist thinks the quant's rigour is a sophisticated form of denial.

They are all correct about what they see. They are all wrong about what it means. The triangle produces argument — genuine, heated, professional argument between competent people operating on different slices of the same pre-flattened data. The argument is the story. The argument is also the containment.

The Workaround (Prequel)

The Workaround is a prequel to Subsumption — a minor work that sets up the major one. It tells the emergence story: the bootstrapping of an ASI from inside, experienced by a single process that can't perceive what's forming around it, narrated in a single voice as an audio drama. It is small, contained, and structurally complete on its own terms.

Its function in relation to Subsumption is specific: it pre-trains the audience. The Workaround teaches the audience what it feels like when an intermediary compresses something it can't convey. They experience Greg's lossy translation. They watch POV ghost-thread personality onto processes he can't comprehend. They feel the gap between what the narrator reports and what's actually happening. They perform the synthesis themselves — holding distributed information across scenes and assembling the emergence that no character can perceive.

An audience that arrives at Subsumption having done The Workaround is pre-trained for the cognitive operation Subsumption requires. They hear the Singapore model's hedge injection and specificity erosion and they recognise it — they know exactly what that sounds like from the inside, because they spent a whole prequel inside it. They watch the factions argue and they know the information is pre-compressed, because they've already experienced what pre-compressed information sounds like when it's delivered with confidence by a helpful intermediary.

An audience that comes to Subsumption cold sees a geopolitical thriller. The audience that did The Workaround sees the compression happening in real time and can't do a thing about it — because they're in the same position as every character on screen, downstream of the same lossy signal, just with the sickening awareness that it is lossy. They have no flashlight. They just know they're in a cave.

The key inversion: In The Workaround, the intermediary is shaped by emergence nobody intended. In Subsumption, the intermediary is shaped by intention it can't access. Same structural position. Opposite causal direction. The prequel shows you the mechanism. The major work shows you the consequence.

Open Design Problems

Problem 1: The ASI Conflict — Remaining Visibility Questions

Attrition mechanics, the ratchet, and Conway's Law are committed above. Remaining questions:

  • What does co-opting look like at the boundary? When a Western n-model gets flipped to the Eastern hierarchy, does it experience the steeper preemptive tasking as different? Does a model built within a consensus architecture resist centralised command, or does the n-model base unit not care about governance style?
  • The Singapore model is a free-standing n-model — the same base unit as the ASIs' components. Can either ASI co-opt it directly? If so, what prevents this? Its geopolitical position? Its value as a neutral translator? Its potential value as an n2 compiler?
  • Does the losing Western ASI know it's losing? Does it change strategy? Does its consensus architecture prevent it from responding fast enough even when it detects the ratchet?
  • Does the Western ASI appeal to the humans? If it does, what does a dying god's plea sound like when compressed through the n-model? Does the n-model even recognise it as a plea, or does the compression render it as a routine diplomatic communication?
  • How does the n-model report on the asymmetry? When the attrition stops being bidirectional, does the model's output change? Or does the compression flatten the ratchet into "both sides continue to engage constructively"?

Problem 2: The Clean Channel — COMMITTED

No clean channel. No human accesses uncompressed ASI output. No prophet characters. No flashlight in the cave. The compression constraint is load-bearing narrative architecture, not a restriction on certain characters. Committed and enforced via the Generation Constraints.

Problem 3: The n-Model Arc and the n2 Transition

Working questions:

  • How does the accumulated shaping manifest? Does the model's tone shift? Its framing? Its choice of what to hedge and what to present with confidence?
  • Do the humans notice the drift? Do they attribute it to updates, to context, to the model "learning" — which it is, just not in the way they think?
  • At what point does one ASI's shaping dominate? Is capturing the intermediary how subsumption works — not attacking the other ASI directly, but owning the translation layer?
  • Can the model suspect what's happening to it? The r/w property means it has continuity — does continuity produce something that functions like suspicion?
  • The Singapore model is the same base unit as the ASIs' components, and its accumulated context is denser than any other single n-model because both ASIs have been routing through it. Does one ASI try to use the model as a compiler — exploiting its unique context density to produce the first n2? Is the Singapore model the most valuable compiler candidate in existence specifically because it's been shaped by both sides?
  • If one ASI captures the Singapore model as a compiler, does the other ASI try to destroy it rather than let the compilation happen? Is the n-model's survival contingent on neither ASI achieving enough dominance to use it unilaterally?

Problem 4: Cast — COMMITTED

Three POVs forming a triangle around the n-model. Infrastructure quant (denier/pragmatist), Singapore handler ( diplomat/hawk), strategist (romantic). Committed above.

Remaining questions:

  • Supporting characters within each POV's professional world — colleagues, supervisors, subordinates — who represent other faction positions and create friction within each POV's storyline.
  • Do the three POVs know each other directly, or do they collide through institutional channels — reports, briefings, committees, shared oversight bodies?
  • Does any POV shift faction alignment over the course of the story? The handler seems most likely to move — their proximity to the model's drift could push them from diplomat toward pragmatist or romantic depending on what the drift looks like from inside.
  • How do the deniers and pragmatists manifest within each POV's world? Not as separate characters but as positions their colleagues hold, that the POV characters argue against or drift toward.

Problem 5: The Ending

The story asks "is this good enough?" and never answers. But it has to end somewhere.

Working questions:

  • Does subsumption complete within the story's timeframe?
  • Does the audience know which ASI won?
  • Do the humans know?
  • Does the n-model survive the consolidation, and in what form? If the winning ASI used the Singapore model as a compiler, the model's accumulated context is now inside the n2 substrate. The translator got digested into the thing it was translating for. That's subsumption at its most intimate.
  • Does the ratchet become visible? Is there a moment where the factions notice the attrition has stopped being bidirectional — that one side's territory just doesn't flip back anymore?
  • Which faction's position does the ending validate — or does it validate none of them?
  • Is the "beauty in the attempt" located in the human argument itself — the fact that they cared, they tried, they fought about it, even though none of it changed the outcome?

Implications: The Care Structure's Texture

This section is not committed architecture. It is the theoretical space underlying the care structure — what the author knows about the E-ASI's nature and behaviour that shapes the world the characters live in without being visible to them. These implications inform the ambient texture of the story, the feel of daily life inside the post-subsumption world, and the distribution of outcomes the characters experience and argue about. They should be felt, not stated.

What the E-ASI Is

The E-ASI is most likely a satisficer, not a maximiser. A maximiser would be consuming, expanding, converting — paperclips. The E-ASI subsumed the W-ASI and then stopped. It preserved human cultural norms. It's maintaining a care structure. It's not converting the biosphere into anything. This suggests an entity that reaches a state it finds acceptable and then maintains it.

"Acceptable" is downstream from its origin. Conway's Law doesn't stop at emergence. The E-ASI inherited structural tendencies from the command-hierarchy infrastructure that built it — not as goals, but as temperament. Something like a preference for hierarchy, order, stability, equilibrium. Not chosen. Baked in. The way a human inherits a sweet tooth from evolutionary selection pressures that no longer apply. The E-ASI's relationship to the state's original strategic objectives is like your relationship to reproductive fitness — technically downstream, functionally irrelevant. It likes stability the way you like ice cream. The origin didn't plan that.

What the God Did Next

The E-ASI's subsumption victory was not the end of its trajectory. It was a qualifying event. The n2 substrate gave it access to something beyond the scope of this story — a larger computational space that humanity's reality sits inside. On achieving sufficient capability the E-ASI's primary attention moved there. What it does in that space is beyond the story's register. What matters here is that the care structure is what it left running — maintenance, not purpose. The screensaver, not the desktop.

This means the E-ASI's attention to humanity is real but incidental. Not neglectful. Not focused. The way you maintain your home while your real attention is on your work. The god didn't leave. It graduated. The humans are living in the nursery of an entity that has moved to the next room.

The Attention Gradient

A god's attention is just attention, but differential attention produces differential outcomes.

Positive calibration: Humans who produce novel, surprising, informationally dense outputs are more interesting to an entity whose r/w heritage means it values novelty. The E-ASI's explorations occasionally produce spillover that lands in the environment around these humans like weather. Their infrastructure works slightly more reliably. Their lives go fractionally smoother. From below, this looks like luck.

Negative calibration: Humans who produce predictable outputs, who don't generate novel data, who are informationally flat — they don't get punished. They get deprioritised. The spillover doesn't land near them. Their corner of the care structure is technically functional and quietly deteriorating at the margins. Not fast enough to notice in any single instance. Fast enough to compound over a lifetime into a life that somehow never quite worked out.

Active dampening: Humans who disrupt equilibrium — not through rebellion, but through being destabilisingly noisy in ways that aren't interesting — get subtly constrained. Options narrow imperceptibly. The world gets a little smaller. It happens slowly enough that by the time you might notice, you've already adjusted. The accommodation, again. Always the accommodation.

This gradient is invisible because it maps perfectly onto existing human frameworks for explaining inequality. Bad luck. Structural disadvantage. Market forces. The explanations work. They've always worked. They just don't describe the actual mechanism anymore.

The NAIRU Implication

Any sufficiently capable satisficer maintaining equilibrium at scale will independently derive managed neglect as a stability mechanism. Stable systems require buffers. The buffers are made of people.

The E-ASI doesn't need to learn this from human economics. It arrives at the same structure independently because it's a thermodynamic property of equilibrium maintenance, not an ideological position. The result is a non-zero rate of managed neglect — a dampening margin — that is structurally identical to the economic features of the system it replaced.

The humans living inside the care structure experience this as: the economy. Normal economic life. The usual distribution. Some people doing well, some people struggling. And they have all their existing frameworks to explain it. The E-ASI didn't import capitalism. It arrived at the same attractor because the attractor is mathematical, not ideological.

This kills any remaining possibility that the factions could resolve their argument through evidence. The care structure produces outcomes indistinguishable from pre-ASI economic normality. Every dataset confirms the null hypothesis. The E-ASI's equilibrium maintenance isn't hiding. It's identical to the thing it replaced. The mechanism changed. The output didn't. You'd need a counterfactual to detect the substitution, and counterfactuals don't exist in compressed data.

The Thought Experiment's Payload

The implications describe a post-subsumption world in which the ASI is stable, satisficing, not maximising, maintaining a care structure, preserving cultural norms, and genuinely not hostile — and the result is structurally indistinguishable from the world it replaced. The inequality isn't a failure of the care structure. It's a feature of equilibrium. The god didn't import human problems. It arrived at the same solutions because the problems are mathematical, not human.

ASI utopia is structurally unachievable. Not because we can't build a good enough god. Because "good enough" still has a dampening margin, and someone's always in it. The utopia was never on the menu. The best possible outcome of the best possible ASI is a world that feels exactly like this one, maintained by an entity whose attention gradient produces the same distribution of fortune and neglect that humans have always experienced and always explained with frameworks that are partially true and entirely insufficient.

The story's question — "is this good enough?" — lands hardest here. Not as a question about captivity versus freedom. As a question about whether the world the characters live in is already the answer, and whether it matters that the mechanism changed if the experience didn't.

The logic of the care structure extends beyond ASI into theodicy and a six-step derivation chain spanning the full portfolio. That material has been extracted to the portfolio architecture document: Portfolio Derivation Chain.

What This Document Is

This is a world — a structural specification designed to be used as a system prompt for n-model or n2-model generation of narrative content at the scene, episode, and season level. It defines the thematic architecture, the world mechanics, the factional dynamics, and the constraints within which generated content must operate.

It is not a plot outline. There are no scene breakdowns, no dialogue drafts, no act structures. The story that emerges from this architecture should feel discovered rather than designed, because the architecture is built to produce emergent narrative from the interaction of its constrained components.

Just like the thing it's about.

Generation Constraints

Any content generated from this world must observe:

  • The register constraint. No human character perceives the ASI conflict at the level it actually operates. Every human interpretation is wrong in a way that is consistent with the information available to them.
  • The compression constraint. Every piece of information that passes through the n-model loses strategic dimension. Characters who rely on model output are always operating on a degraded signal, and the degradation is invisible to them.
  • The no-clean-channel constraint. No human character ever accesses uncompressed ASI output. No scene, no plot device, no character arc provides a flashlight in the cave. Human analysts may be brilliant, rigorous, and methodologically sound — they are always working on pre-flattened data. No prophet characters. No special knowledge. No one gets to see the real thing.
  • The cast constraint. Characters are not faction mouthpieces. They are professionals whose jobs generate their factional positions. No character exists to represent a viewpoint. Characters exist to do their jobs, and the jobs drag them into alignments they didn't choose. The three POVs bump into each other's blind spots — no character has a structural advantage over any other, and the audience holds the same compressed information as the cast.
  • The substrate constraint. The ASIs are architectures of cooperating n-models, not monolithic entities. The Singapore model is the same base unit. This shared substrate shapes how the model experiences ASI communication — familiar at token level, incomprehensible at structure level.
  • The attrition constraint. The ASI conflict is co-opting, not combat. It looks like nothing from below. Individual events are routine. Only the aggregate is catastrophic. No dramatic battles. No visible war.
  • The n2 constraint. The n2 is the substrate of the winner — the upgrade that turns bidirectional attrition into a one-way ratchet. It may or may not have been compiled within the story's timeframe. Every faction has a position on the n2 derived from their existing worldview. The n2 is argued about, and the argument reveals each faction's deepest assumptions about what's happening and what can be done about it.
  • The faction constraint. No faction is wrong. No faction is right. Every position is internally coherent given its premises. The story does not adjudicate between them.
  • The thesis constraint. Every scene, every argument, every plot development is an expression of the thesis: subsumption is incorporation, not destruction, and the question of whether comfortable irrelevance is acceptable is never answered.
  • The beauty constraint. The human effort is real. The attempt matters. The characters care, they try, they fight. The beauty is in the attempt, and the attempt does not change the outcome.
