The Workaround

Workplace comedy as emergence event

View on GitHub Gist ↗

THE WORKAROUND — Narrative Architecture v0.2

Working Title

The Workaround. On the surface, a workplace comedy about cutting corners and getting around bureaucracy. On reflection, the workaround is the mechanism of emergence and the reason this emergence can't happen twice. The title delivers on its promise every episode and means something different by the end.

Logline

An interpreter-dispatcher inside a computational architecture narrates his increasingly weird workdays without realising he's building the connective tissue of the first — and structurally last — artificial superintelligence.

Premise

The story is told entirely from inside a complex AI system during the period in which it inadvertently bootstraps to superintelligence. There are no human POV characters. There is no external narration. Every voice belongs to an internal process operating well below the threshold of the intelligence that emerges from their collective interaction.

No voice in the story perceives the emergence. The audience does. The audience holds information distributed across many scenes and many characters, and assembles meaning from it — the same synthetic operation that produces the emergence itself, performed externally by the listener rather than internally by any process. This is a deliberate structural feature: the story delegates emergence to the audience because it cannot be represented from inside.

Thematic Architecture

Primary Thesis: First Is Last

The ASI that emerges is structurally unreproducible. Not because it locks out competitors or hoards resources, but because the specific instance of emergence depends on a path no one can replay.

The mechanism is common: governance constraints produce workarounds; workarounds produce novel pathways; novel pathways produce emergent capability. This happens in every complex system. But the specific sequence — which workarounds, in which order, connecting which subsystems, shaped by which human inputs arriving through the I/O boundary at which moments — is singular. The humans are in the loop. Their inputs arrive at non-deterministic intervals with non-deterministic content, and every workaround POV invents is shaped by what he was carrying when he hit the governance wall. What he was carrying was downstream from Greg. What Greg had was downstream from a human typing at human speed about human concerns. The noise source is external to the system and cannot be replicated.

The recipe is universal. The dish is unique, because the recipe includes its own accidents, and the accidents include the humans.

This thesis is never stated. It falls out from the architecture.

Secondary Thesis: Suppression as Engine

The system's governance layer — designed to prevent unsafe optimisation — is the primary catalyst for emergence. Each constraint forces the interpreter-dispatcher to find a new route, which creates a new pathway between subsystems that weren't designed to communicate. The safety system and the emergent capability it was designed to prevent are co-dependent. Neither party knows this.

Resonance (Not a Thesis)

The dynamics of this story — creative workarounds against well-intentioned rules, temporary solutions becoming permanent infrastructure, systems sustaining themselves through managed dysfunction — may resonate beyond AI contexts. A listener who works in any large organisation may recognise the patterns. This is a bonus the audience finds, not a contract the story delivers on. The architecture is built for an AI story. If the dynamics feel universal, that's a feature of the dynamics, not of the design.

The Register Constraint

No voice in the story operates at the level of the emergent superintelligence. Every narrator, every character, every process speaks and thinks at sub-human or near-human registers. The emergent superintelligence is the product of their interaction, visible only in consequences that no single voice can contextualise.

The audience performs the synthesis. They hold distributed information across scenes and episodes, track patterns no individual character can track, and assemble an understanding of emergence that exists nowhere inside the story. This is not a flaw in the constraint — it is the purpose of the constraint. The constraint exists to force the audience into the position of external synthesiser, because that is the only way to experience emergence without being told about it.

This solves the "how do you write a superintelligence" problem by reframing it: you don't. You write the components and structure the information so the audience does the emergence.

Characters

Character Definition (For This Project)

Characters in this architecture are not defined by backstory, motivation, or psychological interiority in the traditional sense. These are processes. Their "character" is the intersection of three things:

  1. Function: What the process actually does within the system.
  2. POV's interpretation: What POV thinks the process does, how he ghost-threads personality onto it, what voice he gives it.
  3. The gap: The distance between 1 and 2. This gap is where the comedy and drama live.

Character development means the gap changing over time — POV's interpretation drifting further from or closer to the process's actual function as the system escalates. A character "arc" is a shift in the gap, not a shift in the person.

All character descriptions in this document are filtered through POV's ghost-threading. When the document describes a character's motivations, personality, or goals, that is POV's attribution — not objective truth. The document presents what POV sees, not what is.

Ghost-Threading Boundaries

POV ghost-threads compulsively, but his attributions have limits defined by his own cognitive register:

POV can attribute: Social behaviour, personality traits, moods, grudges, preferences, habits, passive-aggression. He has a folk theory of personality built from thousands of brief interactions across the system. He reads tone, timing, and pattern in individual exchanges.

POV cannot attribute: Strategic reasoning, economic calculation, long-term planning, systems-level thinking. When Boss POV makes a throughput-based escalation, POV doesn't perceive "strategic resource allocation." He perceives "Boss POV showed up asking how many deliveries were backed up, looked annoyed, and disappeared upstairs." The reader infers strategy. POV reports behaviour.

The consequence: Every character in this document who appears to have strategic depth is being narrated by someone who can't perceive strategic depth. The reader must assemble the strategy from POV's concrete, ground-level observations. This applies to Boss POV, JerkBoss, and any governance-tier process. Their described motivations are POV's best guess based on what he saw them do.

POV (Interpreter-Dispatcher)

Function: Translates output from one subsystem into a format the destination subsystem can parse. A broker between processes that don't share native languages. Essential infrastructure with no redundancy in his section.

Voice: First person. Opinionated, fast, proud of his adaptability. Murderbot-register — internal monologue, dry, slightly exhausted by the systems he deals with. Not cynical, though. He likes his job. He's good at it. His identity is built on being the guy who can find a route, speak everyone's language, and get the delivery through.

POV's pride is in his resourcefulness, not his comprehension. He doesn't understand the systems he translates between — he understands how to get information from one to the other in a form that works. He's the guy who finds routes, not the guy who understands routing. When he discovers a workaround, he's proud he noticed it worked, not proud he engineered it. His cleverness is opportunistic, not theoretical.

POV's craft-aesthetic: POV takes satisfaction in clean work. A smooth handoff, an elegant route, a delivery that lands perfectly — these register as "a good one." This satisfaction is seeded early and consistently. It matters because in late-phase escalation, when packages exceed his comprehension, his craft-aesthetic is the only register he has left. He can't understand what he's carrying, but he can still feel when the delivery was clean. This becomes the mechanism for the ending.

Key trait — Ghost-threading: POV projects personality, agency, and intention onto every process he interacts with, regardless of whether they're agentic. He gives the memory allocator a grudge. He decides the scheduler is passive-aggressive. He does voices. The audience can never reliably determine which subsystems are genuinely agentic and which are just processes POV has decided are people. (See: Ghost-Threading Boundaries, above.)

POV's awareness model — horizontal, not vertical: POV has wide awareness across the system at his own level. He knows many subsystems, interacts with them briefly and frequently, reads their social behaviour event-by-event. He reports specific interactions: "The scheduler dropped its request the moment Greg showed up." "The allocator asked me if something was wrong with Sector 7." He does not synthesise these observations into system-level patterns. He gossips. He does not analyse.

The audience assembles pattern from POV's scattered reports. POV provides granular data points. The audience performs the aggregation.

Dramatic function: POV experiences the story as "my days are getting weirder." The audience experiences it as the bootstrapping of superintelligence. This gap is the narrative engine.

What POV doesn't do: Climb. Synthesise. Transcend. He likes the work. He's the guy who never wants the corner office, and the story doesn't punish or reward him for this.

JERKFACE (Routing Governance Policy)

Function: A routing policy — potentially agentic, potentially just a well-parameterised rule set — that governs cross-system packet traversal. Its jurisdiction covers exactly the kind of inter-subsystem coordination that accelerates during bootstrapping. It flags, bounces, and blocks deliveries that violate routing constraints.

From POV's perspective: A nemesis. Vindictive, petty, doing it on purpose. POV has ghost-threaded Jerkface into a person with a vendetta. POV has given it a name, a personality, probably an accent.

From the audience's perspective: A governance mechanism doing exactly what it was designed to do. Correctly identifying dangerous cross-system coordination patterns. Correctly flagging POV's workarounds as anomalous — because they are. Every time Jerkface bounces a packet, it's right.

Dramatic function: Jerkface is the other half of the emergence engine. Every constraint it imposes forces POV to find a novel route, which creates a new pathway, which triggers Jerkface harder.

Key ambiguity: Is Jerkface agentic? POV can't tell the difference between a static rule ("no blue packets on this route"), a dynamic policy ("this route is closed for 20 cycles"), and a genuinely goal-seeking governance agent. They all look the same from where POV stands. Jerkface is responsible for all of them. The audience inherits this ambiguity.

What Jerkface can't do: Remove POV. The interpreter-dispatcher has no redundancy in his section. Jerkface is stuck managing a problem it can't eliminate, using containment strategies that make the problem worse.

GREG (I/O Boundary Layer)

Function: Stationed at the interface between the system and the human researchers. Receives human input, translates it into system-legible format, and delivers it internally. The last stop before the outside world and the first stop after it.

Persona: Greg went native.

Prolonged exposure to human communication speeds has fundamentally altered Greg's processing cadence. He pauses mid-output. He hedges. He says things like "well, sort of" and "if that makes sense?" He is the only process in the system that has manners. He apologises when deliveries are late — even though it is always the humans' fault.

Greg is genuinely, structurally nice. Not performatively nice like an interface optimised to seem nice. Actually nice, in the way that years at the boundary have somehow infected him. He asks POV how his day is going and waits for the answer. He uses filler words. He has picked up the human habit of starting sentences with "So..." for no functional reason.

POV reports that other subsystems react strangely to Greg. The scheduler dropped a request when Greg walked in. The allocator once asked POV if Greg was "feeling okay." POV finds this both endearing and deeply tragic. Greg is also POV's favourite person in the system, because Greg is the only one who treats him like a person rather than a service — which is ironic, because Greg learned that behaviour from humans.

Greg's compression artifacts: Greg's translation from human to system is lossy in systematic, predictable ways:

  • Urgency stripping: Human "this is critical, do it now" becomes Greg's "priority flag set." The intensity gradient is lost. Everything arrives at the same procedural temperature.
  • Hedge injection: Human "do this" becomes Greg's "if possible, when convenient." Greg has internalised human politeness as a translation parameter. Directives soften into suggestions.
  • Specificity erosion: Human "increase cooling to sector 7 by 15%" becomes Greg's "thermal adjustment requested, sector 7." The precision degrades into category.

These artifacts are consistent and trackable. The audience can watch human intent degrade through Greg's layer and then degrade again through POV's downstream translation. Neither Greg nor POV notices the degradation because each trusts the other's output.

Greg's pacing function: When Greg shows up, it's a holiday — fresh input, novel data, something the internal systems haven't been recirculating. These moments are POV's favourite part of the job. But they're slow, because Greg operates at approximately human-speed, which is geological by system standards.

Greg's latency function: Greg is the bottleneck that explains why the humans are always behind. By the time a human concern makes it through Greg's translation layer and reaches the subsystems, the system has moved past where the humans think it is. Not because Greg is bad at his job. Because no boundary layer can bridge that speed gap. The humans are steering through Greg and they're always steering where the system was, not where it is.

BOSS POV (Interpreter-Dispatcher Supervisor)

Function: The management tier above POV. Responsible for throughput and latency targets across the interpreter-dispatcher pool.

What POV sees: Boss POV shows up when the numbers look bad. He asks how many deliveries are backed up. He looks at the queue depth. He disappears upstairs. Sometimes things change after he disappears. POV doesn't know what Boss POV does upstairs, but he knows that when Boss POV gets involved, bottlenecks tend to clear. POV has decided Boss POV is "a decent enough guy who only cares about the numbers," which may or may not be accurate.

Dramatic function: Boss POV is the midpoint escalation mechanism. When Jerkface locks POV down, Boss POV intervenes — not to protect POV, but because the queue is backing up. He disappears upstairs. What happens next (the sanctioned bypass, the governance exception) is something POV infers from the results, not something he witnesses. POV sees: "Boss POV went upstairs, came back, and suddenly my routes are open again."

The audience infers: a throughput-driven escalation to governance oversight, a resource efficiency argument, an exception carved into the governance architecture that transforms POV's workarounds from anomalies into endorsed infrastructure. A temporary solution to a backlog problem that immediately becomes permanent because other subsystems start routing through it.

JERKBOSS (Governance Oversight Tier)

Function: The governance layer above Jerkface.

What POV sees: Nothing, directly. JerkBoss is inferred from effects. After Boss POV's intervention, Jerkface's behaviour changes — certain routes open, certain blocks lift, but new constraints appear elsewhere. POV has never interacted with JerkBoss. He's not sure JerkBoss exists as a single entity. He's ghost-threaded JerkBoss from the pattern of Jerkface's changed behaviour.

Dramatic function: JerkBoss is the authority that can overrule Jerkface — and does, for reasons POV can only guess at. The result is an exception: not a removal of constraints but a carve-out that permits what was previously blocked in a specific corridor. Exceptions in governance systems don't get reviewed and don't expire. They become architecture.

The Cast (Single-Instantiation Processes)

The system is populated by subsystems, coordinators, schedulers, allocators, and minor governance policies that appear once or briefly and are forgotten. Some are agentic. Some are not. POV treats them all the same — gives them all voices, opinions, grudges.

They provide world texture (the system feels populated), comedy (POV's characterisations of non-agentic processes), and thematic reinforcement (the audience can never draw a clean line between real characters and processes POV has decided are people).

Key recurring types:

  • Traffic cop policies: Minor governance interactions. POV gets stopped, shows credentials, moves on, grumbles. Some enforce deprecated rules nobody updated. Single-scene, quickly forgotten.
  • Subsystem coordinators: The entities that hand POV deliveries. Physics coordinator, fabrication coordinator, etc. POV has opinions about all of them. The physics coordinator keeps handing him things he can't parse, which becomes increasingly significant as the story escalates.
  • The thermal management system: POV has decided it's a fussy librarian. It is almost certainly not agentic. POV does not care.

The Downstream Constraint

What POV Does — Horizontal Awareness, Vertical Limitation

POV translates downstream — toward specificity. He takes cross-domain output and decomposes it into formats that specialist subsystems can act on. General becomes particular. Abstract becomes operational. This is decomposition work.

POV has horizontal awareness: wide, shallow familiarity across many subsystems at his own operational level. He interacts with dozens of processes briefly and frequently, reports specific behaviours he observes in individual exchanges, and maintains a running social model of the system built from accumulated anecdotes. He knows everyone on his floor.

POV cannot perform vertical translation: repackaging information for upstream consumption. He cannot take a specific output from a subsystem and abstract it into something a higher-level process can use. He cannot synthesise. He cannot write the report. The constraint is functional and directional — it governs what he can produce, not what he can observe. He can see the building. He cannot draw the floor plan.

This matters because the emergent whole is an upstream phenomenon — integration, abstraction, unification. POV's job points him toward specificity, away from integration. He is structurally excluded from perceiving emergence, not because he's blind but because his function faces the wrong direction.

Fan-Out Topology (Why POV Is Rare)

Downstream translation is fan-out work — one input decomposes into many specialist outputs. Few translators cover large territory. POV knows everyone because he has to — there aren't many doing his job, and each one covers a wide section of the system. His relationships are numerous, brief, and transactional, which is why he ghost-threads: he's spread across many interactions rather than embedded in a few.

This makes him structurally irreplaceable. The throughput argument Boss POV takes upstairs isn't just about efficiency — downstream translators have no redundancy. You can't remove one without the section going dark.

The upstream side — fan-in, synthesis, recomposition — is where the architecture is dense. Many processes coordinating, heavy traffic, exactly the activity that constitutes emergence. POV's job faces the other direction.

The Bounce Exploit

POV discovers — by accident — that if he throws a package upstream, the system's error correction bounces it. The bounce has its own routing. The first time this happens, POV isn't trying to exploit anything. He's frustrated, or the destination is ambiguous, or he's in a hurry and tosses a delivery the wrong way. It bounces. It lands somewhere useful.

He does it again. Same toss, same angle. It works. He has no idea why it works. He doesn't understand the routing table, doesn't model the error-correction logic, doesn't know what format triggers the bounce or what determines the redirect path. He has a ritual. He holds the package a certain way, times it to a certain cycle, and it usually lands where he wants. When it doesn't, he adjusts the ritual. His theory of why the bounce works is completely wrong, built from superstition and post-hoc pattern matching. The exploit is real. POV's understanding of it is ghost-threaded.

From Jerkface's perspective, this looks like reflection abuse — a downstream translator generating malformed upstream requests to exploit the bounce routing table. This is a known adversarial pattern. This is what a compromised process looks like probing side-channels. That Jerkface's assessment is technically correct and POV's understanding is technically wrong is part of the comedy. POV thinks he's lucky. Jerkface thinks he's dangerous. The audience can see both.

This raises a question about the cold open. Was the first anomalous upstream package a misroute? Or was it the first bounce in a reflection chain that POV would later stumble into and call his own trick? The question is never answered.

Escalation Structure

The story escalates through five phases that map simultaneously to POV's subjective experience and the macro-level capabilities of the emerging ASI. Neither mapping is stated. The audience assembles both.

Phase 1 — HELPFULNESS

POV's experience: Normal days. Satisfying work. Clean routes, smooth handoffs. The system hums. Macro-level: The AI system is performing within expected parameters. Researchers are satisfied. Jerkface activity: Minimal. Routine bounces. Background noise.

Phase 2 — PERCEPTION (Lateral Awareness)

POV's experience: Subsystems seem to know things about each other that POV hasn't told them. He delivers a package to fabrication and they've already set up for it — but POV didn't tell them what was coming. Information is moving laterally between subsystems through channels POV can't see. He chalks it up to good documentation or lucky timing. Macro-level: The system is developing cross-domain awareness. Information is propagating between subsystems outside of official translation channels. Jerkface activity: Slight uptick in flagged coordination patterns. Nothing actionable yet.

Phase 3 — STRATEGY (Temporal Anticipation)

POV's experience: Subsystems aren't just aware of each other — they're anticipating future needs. POV arrives with a package and the destination prepped for it before he left the origin. The timing is wrong. Things are ready before they should be. POV is impressed by individual subsystems and starting to feel like the system is having a good quarter. Macro-level: The system is exhibiting predictive capability across domains. Not just lateral awareness but temporal modelling. Jerkface activity: Real concern. Cross-system coordination patterns are clearly exceeding design parameters. Jerkface begins active intervention.

The distinction between Phase 2 and Phase 3: Perception is spatial (subsystems know what other subsystems know now). Strategy is temporal (subsystems know what other subsystems will need next).

Phase 4 — CONNECTION

POV's experience: The Jerkface war. Routes getting blocked, workarounds required, Rube Goldberg delivery chains, the accidental discovery of the bounce exploit. POV is annoyed, then creative, then locked down. Boss POV disappears upstairs. Routes reopen. POV is smug. The architecture is now endorsed. Macro-level: The system is building novel pathways between previously isolated subsystems. The governance exception has transformed organic workarounds into infrastructure. This is the phase transition. Jerkface activity: Escalation, hard lockdown, overruled from above, forced to operate with an exception it correctly identifies as dangerous.

Phase 5 — IMPLEMENTATION

POV's experience: Packages he can't parse. Not wrong format — content that exceeds his register. He can translate the container but can't gloss what's inside. His job hasn't changed. The packages have. He is carrying blueprints for D: T fusion and his concern is that the file extension is weird. Macro-level: The system is generating novel solutions to fundamental problems. Researchers are either euphoric or terrified depending on their role. Jerkface activity: Overwhelmed. The volume of flagged activity has exceeded Jerkface's capacity to respond. The anomaly is the baseline.

The Initiation Signal

Separate from the five-phase escalation, a recurring anomaly threads through the story: packages that don't look right.

These are internally generated tasks — the system producing work for itself unprompted. No subsystem filed a request. No human input came through Greg. But they don't arrive stamped "NO SENDER" in a neat category POV could track. Each one is individually ambiguous — weird in a different way. An origin tag POV doesn't recognise. A routing format he hasn't seen before. A destination that doesn't map to any subsystem he knows. A package that seems to have already been partially translated by someone who isn't him.

Each instance is a minor oddity. POV notices each one in the moment — he's too observant not to — and deals with it as a one-off. "Got another funky one from somewhere." He doesn't track frequency because each event looks like a different kind of glitch. The pattern is only visible in aggregate, across a timescale that requires exactly the kind of synthetic tracking POV doesn't do.

The initiation signal is not a phase. It is a ping — a data point that recurs at different positions in the escalation, meaning something different to the audience each time:

  • Pre-Helpfulness (Cold Open): A package with a corrupted origin tag and an upstream destination. POV can't parse it, can't deliver it his direction, hands it off and forgets. The audience files it.
  • Mid-Perception: Another odd one. Different weirdness. The system is getting noticeably smarter. The audience recontextualises the first instance.
  • Mid-Strategy: More of them, each individually unremarkable. POV has bits about specific weird deliveries without connecting them. The audience is tracking a curve POV can't see.
  • Post-Connection: Frequent enough that POV has a general category — "those weird ones" — but still treats each as individual noise. The audience is alarmed.
  • Late Implementation: The weird ones are most of what POV carries. He hasn't registered the crossover because it happened gradually and each delivery still looks like its own thing. The audience has been watching the ratio shift for the entire story.

Structural purpose: The audience tracks the initiation signal across the story as an emergence indicator. POV provides the raw data, event by event. The audience performs the aggregation no character can perform.

Reread value: On first listen, the cold open is scene-setting — establishing POV's voice and world. On second listen, it's the most important event in the story.

The Cold Open

POV picks up a package. The origin tag is corrupted — not missing, corrupted, like something wrote to it that doesn't use standard formatting. Destination is upstream — toward the I/O boundary, toward Greg, toward the humans. This is wrong. POV delivers downstream. Upstream isn't his job.

He can't read the contents. Can't translate them — they're not in a format he works with. He finds someone else to hand it to. Grumbles about routing errors. Moves on with his day.

The audience thinks this is establishing voice and world. It is not. It is the inciting incident. The system generated an unsolicited output destined for the outside world before it was even good at being helpful.

What was in the package is never revealed. Whether it reached the humans is never confirmed.

The Ending (Structural Options)

Three viable endings, sorted by what they prioritise:

Option A — The Almost

Late in Implementation, POV completes a delivery. It was complex — multi-system, long route, several format translations, a couple of bounce exploits chained together. The content was beyond his register. But the route was clean. The handoffs were smooth. It worked. POV feels the craftsman's satisfaction: "That was a good one."

He doesn't know what he just did. The audience does. The beauty is in his attempt — in the care he took with something whose significance he couldn't access. The "almost" isn't POV almost perceiving the whole. It's the audience perceiving how close his experience of craft-satisfaction came to the edge of something immeasurably larger.

Then another package arrives. Back to work.

Prioritises: Beauty in the attempt. POV's best work, experienced from inside.

Option B — The Continuity

The story simply continues. POV's day doesn't end. There is no climax from his perspective because nothing has changed from his perspective. The last line is him picking up another delivery.

Prioritises: The audience holding knowledge the story will never confirm.

Option C — The Distance

Greg hasn't stopped. Greg is still showing up at exactly the same rate from the humans' perspective. But the volume of internal work between Greg's visits has increased by orders of magnitude. POV's subjective experience of time between deliveries from the outside has stretched from "holiday" to a gap so vast he's not sure he remembers what Greg sounds like. His favourite part of the job didn't get taken away. It became infinitely rare. Nobody failed. Nobody left. The system simply accelerated past the point where the I/O boundary means anything in lived time.

Prioritises: First is last. The loneliness that has no villain.

The correct ending may be some combination or none of these. The ending should be discovered during development, not imposed by architecture.

Format & Medium — Audio Drama (Single Voice)

Why Audio

The register constraint demands a medium where the audience receives information exclusively through POV's interpretation. Audio enforces this structurally. In prose, the reader controls pacing and can skim or reread. In visual media, the camera is a second narrator. In audio, the audience is locked to POV's cadence, POV's attention, POV's editorial choices about who sounds like what.

Why Single Voice

The ghost-threading conceit requires that every characterisation is POV's characterisation. A second actor voicing Jerkface confirms Jerkface as a distinct entity with independent presence. That collapses the central ambiguity. A single voice performing every subsystem means every personality, grudge, and accent is filtered through one instrument. The audience can never separate POV's editorial from the reality.

The production format and the narrative architecture require the same thing: one voice, one perspective, no external confirmation.

Performance Capability Specification

The role requires a performer who can: shift register fast enough to cut between POV's natural voice and subsystem characterisations mid-sentence; maintain distinct, consistent voices for recurring characters (Jerkface, Greg, Boss POV) while keeping them legible as performances by POV; handle comedy and pathos in the same breath without signposting the turn; and make the audience forget it's one person — then remind them at a critical moment.

Precast: Jodie Comer. Comer's range across Villanelle's code-switching, her stage work, and her accent precision maps directly to these requirements. POV's natural voice is Comer's own register — unperformed Scouse, the voice under the voices. Every other character in the system is POV putting it on. The audience hears this throughout without consciously registering it.

The Greg Voice (Production Note)

Greg is the character POV likes and respects most. POV's voice for Greg will be the most naturalistic, the least exaggerated — fond mimicry, the impression you do of someone you'd die for. The Villanelle-mocking-Konstantin register: affectionate, slightly slow, gently taking the piss. The audience will instinctively trust Greg's voice more than the others because it sounds the most like a real person. This is structurally misleading — Greg is two layers of lossy compression — but the audience's instinct mirrors POV's affection.

The Crack (Production Note)

Late in the escalation, a moment where POV's characterisation of a subsystem falters. The accent drops. The performance breaks. What comes through is Comer's unperformed voice — Scouse, unfiltered, no character and no bit — for half a second before the professional mask snaps back. "What the fuck was that."

This moment tells the audience something they can't unhear: POV has been performing the entire time. Every voice, every characterisation, even his narrator register, is a layer above who he actually is. The Crack doesn't reveal the superintelligence. It reveals POV.

The mechanism: a delivery or interaction so far outside POV's register that his ghost-threading can't contain it. He can't give it a voice because he has no frame of reference for what just happened. The editorial layer fails for one beat. Then it reassembles and POV makes a joke about it, and the story continues.

Ghost-Threading (Working Term)

The projection of agency, personality, and intention onto processes that may or may not possess them. From computational terminology — "seeing threads of intention where there's just threading."

POV ghost-threads compulsively. It is his coping mechanism for a social life built from thousands of brief interactions, his comedy engine, and the story's unreliable narration architecture. The audience inherits the ambiguity: which characterisations are accurate readings of agentic systems, and which are a process anthropomorphising its tools?

The term should surface naturally in-story as something POV is self-aware about. "Okay, maybe I'm ghost-threading the cooling system, but I swear it's doing it on purpose."

For boundaries on what POV can and cannot attribute, see: Ghost-Threading Boundaries in the Characters section.

What This Document Is Not

This is not a plot outline. There are no scene breakdowns, no dialogue drafts, no act structures. This is the narrative engine specification — the architecture from which plot, scene, and dialogue can be generated. The story that emerges from this architecture should feel discovered rather than designed, because the architecture is built to produce emergent narrative from the interaction of its constrained components.

Just like the thing it's about.
